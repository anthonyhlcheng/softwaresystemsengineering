Overview

Requirement analysis seeks to answer the question to the client: “What do you want your software to do?” Often the client does not know exactly what they want from their commissioned software developers. Requirements analysis helps the client work out what they want from their product. Requirement analysis can be divided into 3 areas:

• Finding the requirements - This is the stage where the software developers actually ask the clients questions about the required system.

• Analysing the requirements - The developers check if the requirements given are possible. Other aspects that are checked are if the goals set are clear and concise.

• Recording the requirements - Once all the requirements are settled, they are recorded, usually in the form of a list.

While this process is crucial to the success of a system. It can be a difficult process. Large systems may have hundreds or even thousands of requirements. The environment and society also need to be consider as the new system may affect them.

Prototyping

Prototyping could be used to produce an example product that can demonstrate to the client how the system they are asking for may work. This also gives the client a chance to rethink some of their requirements, for example a client may think that some of the functionality they originally wanted is unnecessary after seeing a prototype.

Research

Change oriented Requirements Analysis is a method of analysis that anticipates sudden change in requirements. It does this by separating the requirements into different layers that change at different rates. This is called shearing. The layers are grouped into Patterns or behaviours, functional constraints, policies and non-functional constraints.

A pattern in this context is like an object. It contains behaviours such as getting a person’s age that allow it to achieve a certain goal.

CoRE has five steps:

1. Assigning patterns to goals
The goals identified from the client are then assigned to patterns. An example of this is that the goal for finding a university building by name can be matched to the functionality in the pattern that identifies a university building by several means such as postcode.

2. Refining goals
The goals are broken down into sub goals and then step one is repeated on the sub goals until they cannot be sub divided any further.

3. Identifying constraints
For every pattern assigned, constraints are identified. This is done by asking users of the system what tasks they depend on the system to complete.

4. Identifying policies
The fully refined goals are assigned to policies. For example a goal to assign access rights to staff and visitors in a company would be supported by that company’s access policy.

5. Identifying non-functional constraints.
The last step is identifying non-functional constraints. Non-functional constraints are restrictions on the quality characteristics, for example reliability and usability.

The result is a list of requirements that are separated into four layers.
